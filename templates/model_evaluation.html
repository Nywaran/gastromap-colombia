4.1 Why No Confusion Matrix?

A confusion matrix requires comparing predicted labels vs actual labels (ground truth). Since no ground truth exists for gastronomic tourism potential in Colombia, a confusion matrix cannot be constructed. This is precisely why we chose unsupervised learning.

Instead, we use four alternative validation strategies appropriate for unsupervised scoring models.

4.6 Risk Assessment

Overfitting

Not applicable — unsupervised models do not overfit to training labels since none exist. The scoring formula is deterministic and does not learn from a training set.

Not Applicable

Underfitting

The five-component model captures the main dimensions of gastronomic tourism. Adding more features could improve granularity but would increase complexity without clear benefit.

Low Risk

Data Bias

Larger cities have more registered RNT providers, creating an inherent urban bias. Log transformation partially mitigates this but cannot eliminate it entirely. Rural municipalities with informal gastronomy may be underscored.

Medium Risk

Weight Subjectivity

Component weights reflect domain judgment, not statistical optimization. Different experts might assign different weights. Sensitivity analysis shows this mainly affects mid-tier rankings, not the top or bottom.

Medium Risk

4.7 Future Supervised Extension

The GTPI scores generated by this system can serve as pseudo-labels for future supervised models. Once domain experts validate and refine the top/bottom classifications, a gradient boosting or random forest classifier could be trained to predict GTPI class from raw features — enabling automated scoring of new municipalities or updated datasets without re-running the full pipeline.
